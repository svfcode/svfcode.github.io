Gradient

Vector of partial derivatives — direction of steepest increase and the basis of gradient descent

Mar 13, 2026
2 minute read
ru

The gradient of a function of several variables is the vector of all its partial derivatives. It points in the direction of steepest increase of the function. Gradient descent updates parameters in the direction \( -\nabla f \) to minimize the loss.

Assumes familiarity with partial derivatives.

Definition

For a function \( f(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) \), the gradient \( \nabla f \) is the vector:

\[ \nabla f = \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}, \ldots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \right) \]

The symbol \( \nabla \) (nabla) is the standard notation for the gradient.

Geometric interpretation

The gradient at a point is a vector pointing in the direction of steepest increase of the function. Its magnitude is proportional to the rate of increase in that direction.

Therefore:

  • \( \nabla f \) — direction of steepest increase
  • \( -\nabla f \) — direction of steepest decrease

To minimize \( f \) (e.g., the loss), we move in the direction \( -\nabla f \).

Gradient descent

Gradient descent is an iterative minimization method. At each step:

\[ w_{new} = w_{old} - \alpha \cdot \nabla L(w_{old}) \]

or component-wise:

\[ w_k^{(new)} = w_k^{(old)} - \alpha \cdot \frac{\partial L}{\partial w_k} \]

where:

  • \( \alpha \) — learning rate (step size): too large — we may overshoot the minimum; too small — training is slow
  • \( L \) — loss function

We take a step opposite to the gradient because we want to decrease the loss.

Example: \( f(x, y) = x^2 + xy \)

From the partial derivatives article:

  • \( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 2x + y \)
  • \( \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = x \)

Gradient:

\[ \nabla f = (2x + y,\; x) \]

At point \( (1, 2) \): \( \nabla f = (4, 1) \). To increase \( f \) fastest, move in direction \( (4, 1) \); to decrease it — in direction \( (-4, -1) \).

Connection to the chain rule

In neural networks, the loss depends on the weights through a long chain of computations. To compute \( \nabla L \) for all weights, we use the chain rule and backpropagation — see the dedicated article.

Summary

  • Gradient \( \nabla f \) — vector of partial derivatives
  • Direction of steepest increase — \( \nabla f \), steepest decrease — \( -\nabla f \)
  • Gradient descent — updates \( w := w - \alpha \nabla L \) to minimize loss
