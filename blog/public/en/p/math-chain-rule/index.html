Chain Rule

How to compute derivatives of composed functions — the backbone of backpropagation in neural networks

Mar 13, 2026
3 minute read
ru

The chain rule allows us to compute the derivative of a composition of functions via a product of derivatives. In neural networks, the loss depends on the weights through a chain of layers; backpropagation is an efficient implementation of the chain rule for computing gradients.

Assumes familiarity with derivatives and the gradient.

Single-variable case

If \( z = f(y) \) and \( y = g(x) \), then \( z \) depends on \( x \) through \( y \):

\[ \frac{dz}{dx} = \frac{dz}{dy} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} \]

The derivative “flows” along the chain: from \( z \) to \( y \), then from \( y \) to \( x \).

Example: \( z = y^2 \), \( y = 3x + 1 \). So \( z = (3x+1)^2 \).

\[ \frac{dz}{dy} = 2y,\quad \frac{dy}{dx} = 3 \]\[ \frac{dz}{dx} = 2y \cdot 3 = 6(3x+1) \]

We can verify: \( z = (3x+1)^2 \), so \( \frac{dz}{dx} = 2(3x+1) \cdot 3 = 6(3x+1) \). ✓

Multivariable case

If \( z = f(y_1, \ldots, y_m) \) and each \( y_j = g_j(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \):

\[ \frac{\partial z}{\partial x_i} = \sum_{j=1}^{m} \frac{\partial z}{\partial y_j} \cdot \frac{\partial y_j}{\partial x_i} \]

Sum over all “paths” from \( x_i \) to \( z \) through the intermediate \( y_j \).

Connection to neural networks

A neural network is a chain of layers:

\[ \text{input } x \rightarrow \text{layer 1} \rightarrow h_1 \rightarrow \text{layer 2} \rightarrow h_2 \rightarrow \ldots \rightarrow \text{output } \hat{y} \rightarrow L \]

The loss \( L \) depends on \( \hat{y} \), which depends on the last layer weights, and so on. To get \( \frac{\partial L}{\partial w} \) for an earlier layer weight, we multiply derivatives along all intermediate quantities on the path from \( w \) to \( L \).

Backpropagation

Backpropagation — an algorithm that in one backward pass computes all \( \frac{\partial L}{\partial w} \) for all weights. Idea:

  1. Forward pass — compute each layer’s outputs and save them
  2. Backward pass — start with \( \frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} = 1 \) (or the explicit derivative of loss w.r.t. prediction) and “propagate” the gradient backward through each layer using the chain rule

Efficiency: without backprop we’d have to manually unfold the chain for each weight; with backprop — one pass yields gradients for all weights.

Example: two layers

Let \( \hat{y} = w_2 \cdot h \), \( h = \sigma(w_1 x) \), \( L = \frac{1}{2}(\hat{y} - y)^2 \).

\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial w_2} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial w_2} = (\hat{y} - y) \cdot h \]\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial w_1} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial h} \cdot \frac{\partial h}{\partial w_1} = (\hat{y} - y) \cdot w_2 \cdot \sigma'(w_1 x) \cdot x \]

The gradient “flows” backward: from \( L \) to \( \hat{y} \), to \( h \), to \( w_1 \).

Summary

  • Chain rule — \( \frac{dz}{dx} = \frac{dz}{dy} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} \)
  • For many variables — sum of products over all paths
  • Backpropagation — efficient algorithm for computing gradients along a chain of layers

Concrete example with formulas: linear regression.
