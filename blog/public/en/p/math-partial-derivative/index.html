Partial Derivative

Derivative with respect to one variable while others are fixed — foundation of working with multivariable functions in ML

Mar 13, 2026
3 minute read
ru

A partial derivative is the derivative of a function of several variables with respect to one of them, treating the others as constants. In machine learning, the loss depends on thousands or millions of weights; we need to know how the loss changes when we change each weight separately.

Assumes familiarity with derivatives of single-variable functions.

Definition

For a function \( f(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) \), the partial derivative with respect to \( x_k \) is denoted \( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_k} \) and is computed as the ordinary derivative with respect to \( x_k \), with all other variables held constant.

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_k} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x_1, \ldots, x_k + h, \ldots, x_n) - f(x_1, \ldots, x_k, \ldots, x_n)}{h} \]

The symbol \( \partial \) (curly “d”) emphasizes that only one variable changes; the others are “frozen.”

Example: \( f(x, y) = x^2 + xy \)

Computing \( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \): treat \( y \) as a constant.

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2) + \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xy) = 2x + y \]

Computing \( \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \): treat \( x \) as a constant.

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^2) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(xy) = 0 + x = x \]

Result:

  • \( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 2x + y \)
  • \( \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = x \)

Geometric interpretation

The graph \( z = f(x, y) \) is a surface in 3D. The partial derivative \( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \) at \( (x_0, y_0) \) is the slope of the tangent to the cross-section of the surface by the plane \( y = y_0 \) (we move only along the \( x \)-axis).

Similarly, \( \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \) is the slope of the cross-section by \( x = x_0 \).

Why it matters in machine learning

A neural network has parameters \( w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_n \). The loss function \( L(w_1, \ldots, w_n) \) depends on all weights.

  • \( \frac{\partial L}{\partial w_k} \) — how much the loss increases if we slightly increase \( w_k \) while keeping other weights fixed
  • Negative partial derivative — loss decreases when \( w_k \) increases; positive — it grows
  • To update weights we need all \( \frac{\partial L}{\partial w_1}, \ldots, \frac{\partial L}{\partial w_n} \) — i.e. the gradient (see the gradient article).

Summary

  • Partial derivative \( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_k} \) — rate of change of \( f \) with respect to \( x_k \) with other variables fixed
  • Computed as an ordinary derivative; all variables except \( x_k \) are constants
  • In ML — foundation for updating each weight separately
