Math Analysis — Limits

Limit of a function — foundation of differential calculus and understanding derivatives

Mar 13, 2026
8 minute read
ru

The limit is one of the key concepts in mathematical analysis. Without it, derivatives, continuity, and many optimization algorithms cannot be rigorously defined.

The derivative is a limit. The article on derivatives relies on this concept.

Limits are needed wherever there is an infinite process that converges to something.

What is a limit

Simple explanation

A limit is the number a function “approaches” as the argument gets closer to some point (or to infinity).

  • “Approaches” does not mean “equals” — the function may be undefined at the point itself but still have a limit.
  • The limit describes the behavior of the function “near” the point.

Example. The function \( f(x) = \frac{\sin x}{x} \) is undefined at \( x = 0 \) (division by zero). But as \( x \to 0 \), the ratio \( \frac{\sin x}{x} \) approaches 1. We write:

\[ \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1 \]

Formal definition

ε-δ (epsilon-delta) definition

A number \( L \) is called the limit of a function \( f(x) \) as \( x \to a \) if:

for every \( \varepsilon > 0 \) there exists \( \delta > 0 \) such that whenever \( 0 < |x - a| < \delta \), we have \( |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon \).

In other words: however small we require the “accuracy” \( \varepsilon \) to be, we can always find a neighborhood \( \delta \) around \( a \) where \( f(x) \) differs from \( L \) by less than \( \varepsilon \).

Why we need it

The paradox of Achilles and the tortoise (Zeno, 5th century BC): Achilles races a tortoise. The tortoise gets a head start. Achilles reaches the point where the tortoise was — but the tortoise has moved forward. He runs to the new position — again the tortoise is a bit ahead. Each time a “last segment” remains. It seems Achilles can never catch up.

In reality, he does. The paradox arose because we were adding infinitely many segments and did not know whether such a sum could be finite. The concept of a limit provides the answer: the sum of an infinite sequence of segments can converge to a finite number. The time it takes Achilles to catch the tortoise is the limit of the sum of ever-smaller intervals.

\[ T = t_1 + t_2 + t_3 + \ldots \to \text{finite number} \]

Without limits we cannot rigorously describe convergence of series, derivatives, integrals — or many machine learning algorithms (gradient descent, iteration convergence).

Notation

\[ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \]

read: “the limit of \( f(x) \) as \( x \) approaches \( a \) is \( L \)”.

Limit of a sequence and limit of a function

These are two related but distinct concepts.

Limit of a sequence

A sequence is a function whose argument takes only natural numbers: \( a_1, a_2, a_3, \ldots \), or \( (a_n)_{n=1}^{\infty} \).

A number \( L \) is the limit of the sequence \( (a_n) \) if as \( n \to \infty \) the terms \( a_n \) approach \( L \) arbitrarily closely.

Formally (ε-N): for every \( \varepsilon > 0 \) there exists an index \( N \) such that for all \( n > N \) we have \( |a_n - L| < \varepsilon \).

Notation: \( \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = L \) or \( a_n \to L \) as \( n \to \infty \).

Examples:

  • \( a_n = \frac{1}{n} \): \( \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} = 0 \)
  • \( a_n = \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n \): \( \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = e \)

The argument \( n \) tends to infinity — a sequence has no other “directions” to consider.

Limit of a function

A function \( f(x) \) is defined on a set (e.g., an interval). The argument \( x \) can approach a point \( a \) in different ways: from the left, from the right, along different paths (in the multivariate case).

A number \( L \) is the limit of the function \( f(x) \) as \( x \to a \) if as \( x \) approaches \( a \) (with \( x \neq a \)) the value \( f(x) \) approaches \( L \).

Formally (ε-δ): for every \( \varepsilon > 0 \) there exists \( \delta > 0 \) such that whenever \( 0 < |x - a| < \delta \), we have \( |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon \).

Notation: \( \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \).

Difference from a sequence: \( x \) tends to a finite point \( a \) (or to \( \pm\infty \)), not only to \( +\infty \). All “ways” of approaching \( a \) must be taken into account.

Relation between them

A sequence can be viewed as a function \( f(n) = a_n \) defined on \( \mathbb{N} \). Then the limit of a sequence is a special case of the limit of a function as \( n \to +\infty \). But usually they are distinguished: for a sequence the argument is discrete (\( n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots \)), for a function it is continuous.

Simple examples

1. Constant. \( f(x) = 5 \). For any \( x \to a \), the value \( f(x) = 5 \).

\[ \lim_{x \to a} 5 = 5 \]

2. Linear function. \( f(x) = 2x + 1 \). As \( x \to 3 \) we get \( f(x) \to 7 \).

\[ \lim_{x \to 3} (2x + 1) = 7 \]

3. Undefined at the point, but limit exists. \( f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1} \). At \( x = 1 \) — division by zero. But for \( x \neq 1 \) we can simplify:

\[ \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1} = \frac{(x-1)(x+1)}{x-1} = x + 1 \]

As \( x \to 1 \), the expression \( x + 1 \to 2 \). So:

\[ \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1} = 2 \]

4. No limit. \( f(x) = \frac{1}{x} \) as \( x \to 0 \). For \( x > 0 \) we have \( \frac{1}{x} \to +\infty \), for \( x < 0 \) — \( \to -\infty \). There is no single finite “limit”.

Properties of limits

If \( \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \) and \( \lim_{x \to a} g(x) = M \) exist, then:

Property Formula
Sum \( \lim(f + g) = L + M \)
Difference \( \lim(f - g) = L - M \)
Product \( \lim(f \cdot g) = L \cdot M \)
Quotient (when \( M \neq 0 \)) \( \lim(f/g) = L/M \)
Constant \( \lim(c \cdot f) = c \cdot L \)

These rules allow us to break complex limits into simpler ones.

Important limits

1. First remarkable limit:

\[ \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1 \]

Used in the derivation of the derivative of sine and in many problems.

2. Second remarkable limit (base \( e \)):

\[ \lim_{n \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n = e \approx 2.718 \]

In another form: \( \lim_{x \to 0} (1 + x)^{1/x} = e \).

3. The limit that gives the derivative:

\[ \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} = f'(x) \]

This is the definition of the derivative — the limit of the ratio of the change in the function to the change in the argument.

Limits at infinity

When \( x \to +\infty \) or \( x \to -\infty \), the limit describes the behavior of the function “at large distances”.

Examples:

\[ \lim_{x \to +\infty} \frac{1}{x} = 0,\quad \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{1}{x} = 0 \]

\[ \lim_{x \to +\infty} \frac{1}{x^2} = 0 \]

Polynomials. As \( x \to \infty \), the leading term dominates:

\[ \lim_{x \to +\infty} \frac{2x^3 + x - 1}{x^3} = 2 \]

(divide numerator and denominator by \( x^3 \), lower-order terms tend to zero).

Connection to the derivative

The derivative is a limit:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} \]

Therefore:

  • Finite differences \( \frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x} \) — an approximation when \( \Delta x \) is small but nonzero.
  • The derivative \( f'(x) \) — the exact value of the limit as \( \Delta x \to 0 \).

Formulas like \( (x^n)' = nx^{n-1} \) are obtained as the result of this limit, not as an approximation. For more details, see the article on Derivatives.

Summary

  • Limit — the number a function approaches as the argument approaches a point (or infinity).
  • The limit describes the behavior of the function “near” the point; the function itself may be undefined at that point.
  • Derivative — a special case of a limit: \( f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} \).
  • Understanding limits is needed for a rigorous understanding of differential calculus and optimization algorithms (gradient descent, convergence).
